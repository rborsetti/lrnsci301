## Slide 2 · Intro / Context
_30 sec · cumulative 0:43 · Value to students' lives (Context is age-appropriate and valuable)_
_Visual: images/03-briefing.png_

Apollo 13 is age-appropriate, emotionally compelling, and historically a story of regular math saving lives. For a 9th-grader on the bridge between general math and algebra, this is the moment "what is *x*" stops being abstract. Every equation in the game ties to a system on the ship that needs repairing. The stakes are cooperative, a crew to save, instead of a test grade to chase. The math is identical. The meaning is not.

## Slide 4 · Standard + Target Audience
_25 sec · cumulative 1:10 · Standard + portion of standard_

Our full Common Core standard covers linear equations and inequalities in one variable. We chop it down to the portion the game actually trains: isolating a variable through multiplication and division. Our learners are early high school algebra students, still bridging from general math. Each ship system in the game is one equation form drawn from this chopped standard.

---

## Slide 5 · The Knowledge (4 Learning Outcomes)
_30 sec · cumulative 1:40 · Knowledge clearly explained_

Four pieces of knowledge, each generalizable beyond algebra class. One: find the value of a base unit. Two: use grouping to deduce a base unit through division. Three: use replication to find a base unit through multiplication. Four: interpret a base unit across both sides of an equation. Every system in the game maps onto one of these four, so by the end of the mission the student has practiced all four in context.

## Slide 7 · The Game
_35 sec · cumulative 2:12 · Cohesive explanation (game can be started quickly)_
_Visual: images/02-mission-control.png_

Walk through the loop in one breath. Teacher opens Mission Control on the laptop, students join from one phone per crew with the code, every student gets a logbook. For each system: copy numbers from console to logbook, fold the paper strip to find one unit, type the answer to restore the system. Win condition: all systems restored. Two modes let the teacher dial the energy: live race or low-stakes story.

---

## Slide 8 · Sample Turn
_55 sec · cumulative 2:52 · (reinforces cohesive explanation; the exemplar called sample turns "highly effective")_
_Visual: images/04-phone-task.png (panels 1 and 4) and images/07-logbook-fold-cut.png (panels 2 and 3), with overlay labels on each panel._

Walk through the turn. System 04: the mainframe needs power, two wires must share it equally, total power is given. On the console the team sees the broken system and the numbers. Students copy those numbers onto the matching System 04 page in their logbook. Now the math becomes physical. They fold the paper strip in half, crease it, cut along the crease. Each piece is one wire's power. That cut piece is x. They write x on the page, type it on the console, the system restores. Crucially the math wasn't done on the phone. It was discovered on paper.

## Slide 11 · Design Argument Table
_50 sec · cumulative 4:07 · Mechanics → Gameplay → Experience_

This is the design argument. Each row is one of the four learning outcomes from earlier, traced through mechanics, gameplay, and experience. LO one, find the base unit: the experience is finding a ship's speed, the gameplay is solving inverse operations, the mechanic is the system math itself. LO two, division: the experience is rewiring with a known group count. LO three, multiplication: replicating energy pulses to rebuild the power core. LO four, variable on both sides: applying algebra to real-world contexts like oxygen and velocity. The mechanic in every row is the wording of a scenario that forces the right relationship to surface. Mechanics produce the gameplay; gameplay produces the experience. ``

## Slide 12 · Evidence
_40 sec · cumulative 4:39 · Evidence the game can teach (pre/post + in-game = +1 bonus)_
_Visual: images/05-phone-hint-steps.png (small, in the right column under the in-game list)_

Four pre/post questions diagnose the four LOs directly. They map one-for-one: coefficients and variables (LO1), division as inverse (LO2), multiplication as inverse (LO3), variable on both sides (LO4). During play the server records attempts per system, hint usage (gated so it only appears after a wrong answer, which means usage itself is data), time per system, and we keep the physical logbook. The rubric notes in-game measures are a one-point bonus. We're claiming it.

---

## Slide 13 · Inclusion
_35 sec · cumulative 5:14 · Different student types participate (shy, doesn't like schoolwork, backseat in groups)_
_Visual: images/11-story-mode-hidden.png_

Four design moves for inclusion. First, one logbook per student, not per team, so every kid is hands-on. The classic group-work failure where one student does everything is structurally blocked. Second, named crew roles give every student a job. Third, Story mode hides cross-team progress so a struggling team is never watched. Fourth, hints and full step-by-step solutions exist but they only appear after a genuine wrong attempt. That makes them a safety net, not a shortcut, and removes the shame of asking.

## Slide 15 · Fun + Ethics
_40 sec · cumulative 5:51 · Clear fun mechanics AND mechanics ethically justified (two rubric items on one slide)_
_Visual: images/08-race.png (top left) and images/09-splashdown.png (top right)_

Fun first. Every device counts down together to LIFTOFF. In competition mode live race bars cross the room. When the last system is fixed the ship splashes down on the wall projector. Now the ethics. The race is opt-in, not the default. Story mode is offered as the gentler first run. Hints are unlimited, never paywalled. Story mode never shows a leaderboard so struggling students aren't watched. And even in competition, every crew gets the same "welcome home" closing. The fun is real. The pressure is calibrated.
