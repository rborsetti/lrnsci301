Mission briefing

Inspired by Apollo 13

"Houston, we've had a problem."

April 13, 1970. Two hundred thousand miles from Earth, an oxygen tank ruptures aboard Apollo 13. Power drops, coordinates scramble, oxygen tanks scatter, the navigation scanner glitches. Every system shows incomplete information, and the only way to repair each one is to solve for the missing value: the unknown, x.

Today, the crew is you. Every team races the same 10 systems independently. First crew to splashdown wins.

The console and the logbook work together. Each crew has a printed Flight Engineer's Logbook. The console shows a broken system; the crew copies its numbers onto the matching logbook page, folds and cuts the paper strip by hand until they can see one unit, then enters the answer on the console. Same numbered system on both. Hints and step-by-step are there if a crew is stuck after working it on paper.

What you used

  • Isolating x by adding or subtracting the same value from both sides.
  • Multiplying or dividing both sides when the variable was scaled (2x, x/3, …).
  • Two-step equations with coefficients and constants (3x + 2 = 20).
  • Variables on both sides, collecting like terms to solve x − 15 = 2x − 45.

The real Apollo 13

In April 1970, NASA flight controllers and the three-astronaut crew solved life-or-death calculations with pencils, slide rules, and the math they already knew: power budgets, burn durations, course corrections. Every astronaut returned safely. You just walked the same path, one equation at a time.
